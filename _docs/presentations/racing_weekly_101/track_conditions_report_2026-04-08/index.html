What Does a Good 4 Really Mean?

The purpose of this week's content is to educate owners and new racing followers about NZ track conditions and why the rating matters before race day.

Overview

Track conditions look simple on paper, but that small number beside the race meeting can tell you a lot about how a race may unfold. In New Zealand, tracks are rated on a 10-point scale, from firmer and faster surfaces through to wetter, more testing ground.

For a beginner or owner learning the game, this matters because different surfaces can influence race times, tactics, and which horses are likely to perform best on the day. A horse that feels comfortable on a firm track may not be nearly as effective when the ground is softer and more tiring.

Lower number = firmer, faster track. Higher number = softer, wetter track.

Key Points To Teach

  • New Zealand uses a 10-point track-rating scale aligned with Australia.
  • Firm 1-2 means a firmer, faster surface.
  • Good 3-4 is generally considered ideal racing ground.
  • Soft 5-7 means there is more give in the surface.
  • Heavy 8-10 means wetter, more testing conditions.
  • Track managers assess the surface with a penetrometer plus visual inspection.
  • Older terms like Fast, Dead, and Slow were replaced. The old Dead 4 is now Good 4.

Why This Matters For Owners

Owners do not need to become track experts overnight, but understanding the rating helps them make more sense of race-day commentary. It explains why the same horse can look sharp one week and much less effective the next, even when the distance or class has not changed dramatically.

It also gives context around strategy. Softer tracks can make races more testing late, while firmer tracks often suit speed and rhythm. That is why trainers, jockeys, and form students all pay attention to the ground before a horse steps out.
